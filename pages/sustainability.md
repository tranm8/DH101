# Sustainability & Ethics

## My Research Summary

AI does not exist in a digital vacuum; it is a massive "extractive system". From the physical
excavation of the earth to the consumption of millions of gallons of water for cooling, AI poses
severe challenges to planetary sustainability.

## Personal Reflection

● **Habits:** I use AI frequently to support data analysis for my SES and Economics research
at Denison University.

● **Estimated Impact:** Based on my weekly query volume, I am contributing to several liters
of "invisible" water consumption.

● **Willingness to Change:** I am willing to stop using AI for trivial tasks and optimize my
prompts to reduce server load.

● **What I Can’t Change:** I cannot stop using AI entirely because it is an essential tool for
processing the complex economic models required by my academic curriculum.

## Systemic vs. Individual

● **Individual:** We can optimize habits, but individual effort is dwarfed by the scale of the
system.

● **Systemic:** True change requires holding tech companies accountable for the entire
product lifecycle, from mining to e-waste.

● **Responsibility:** Big Tech must move beyond "green" metaphors and implement 24/7
carbon-free energy and total mineral transparency.

## Ethical Considerations

For me, the ethics of AI are not only about whether the final output is correct or beautiful. They are also about what the technology hides. AI systems depend on data, human labor, minerals, water, electricity, and global supply chains. This means that every AI-generated image, text, or answer is connected to real environmental and social costs.

One major ethical issue is invisible labor. AI often appears automatic, but many people contribute to its operation, including data labelers, content moderators, miners, factory workers, and users whose data becomes part of training systems. If we only focus on the final AI output, we may forget the people who make the system possible.

Another ethical issue is environmental responsibility. AI may look clean because it happens on a screen, but it requires data centers, cooling systems, hardware, and energy. This makes sustainability an important part of responsible AI use.

In my portfolio, I try to use AI critically rather than passively. I document when I use AI, revise its suggestions, check whether the ideas fit my own argument, and take responsibility for the final work. To me, ethical AI use means asking: who benefits, who is harmed, what is hidden, and how can we make these systems more visible and accountable?
