# Curriculum

This page explains the learning path behind my DH101 portfolio. Instead of treating each Make as a separate assignment, I see the course as a curriculum about creativity, technology, ethics, labor, environment, and human responsibility in the age of AI.

DH101: *Critical Making in the Age of AI* taught me that digital work is never only technical. Every image, map, network, game, or website also carries questions about identity, authorship, power, bias, labor, and ecology.

---

## Course Focus

This course explored how humans create with artificial intelligence while also questioning the systems behind it. Through weekly Makes, readings, reflections, and website building, I learned to treat AI not only as a tool, but as a cultural, political, and environmental system.

The main question guiding my portfolio is:

> What does it mean to be human in the age of AI?

My answer is that being human means making creatively, thinking critically, documenting honestly, and taking responsibility for the consequences of technology.

---

## Skills I Developed

### Critical Making

I learned that making is a form of thinking. Each project was not only a finished object, but also an argument. My selfie, comic, map, network, and game all helped me explore how AI shapes identity, storytelling, labor, and power.

### Digital Storytelling

I practiced turning ideas into digital experiences through comics, Twine games, visual artifacts, and web pages. I learned that storytelling can happen through images, choices, maps, links, and interface design.

### AI Literacy

I learned to use AI as a collaborator for brainstorming, editing, coding support, and revision. At the same time, I learned not to accept AI outputs passively. I had to check, revise, question, and take responsibility for the final work.

### Web Design

I built this website using GitHub Pages, Markdown, HTML, CSS, and VS Code. I learned how to organize pages, create navigation, embed projects, improve accessibility, and design a portfolio that can be read on both mobile and desktop screens.

### Ethical Reflection

I learned to ask ethical questions about AI: Who made this system possible? Whose data is used? Whose labor is hidden? What environmental resources are consumed? Who benefits, and who is harmed?

## Portfolio Makes

### Week 3: When AI Makes Me Too Perfect

This Make explored AI, identity, beauty standards, and authenticity through an AI-generated selfie. It helped me think about what AI can imitate and what it cannot understand about a person’s memory, flaws, and humanity.

### Week 4: The Everyday Joke Machine

This comic used everyday Vietnamese conversation to explore humor, friendship, language, and storytelling. It showed me that digital storytelling does not need to be dramatic to be meaningful.

### Week 7: AI’s Death Geography

This map traced the hidden infrastructure behind AI, including minerals, data centers, labor, and environmental cost. It helped me see AI as a global material system rather than a weightless “cloud.”

### Week 8: Cartography of the Invisible

This network visualization showed how AI is connected to corporations, resource extraction, labor, waste, and power. It made visible the relationships that are often hidden behind clean technology narratives.

### Week 10: The AI Boss Interview

This Twine game explored leadership, automation, profit, ethics, and human accountability. It taught me that games can make arguments through rules, choices, and consequences.

### Week 12: The Cloud Has a Body

This ecology project focused on the environmental cost of AI, including water, energy, minerals, hardware, and e-waste. It connected my interests in Economics, Environmental Studies, and responsible technology.

---

## What This Curriculum Taught Me

By the end of the course, I learned that AI is not separate from human life. It is connected to bodies, labor, land, data, emotion, institutions, and ecosystems. I also learned that creativity is not only about making something beautiful. It is also about asking better questions.

This portfolio represents my growth as a critical maker. I learned how to use AI, but also how to challenge it. I learned how to build a website, but also how to make that website communicate a clear argument. Most importantly, I learned that responsible technology requires transparency, care, and human judgment.

---

## Connection to My Future Work

As a student interested in Economics, Finance, and Environmental Studies, this course helped me think about technology through both social and environmental responsibility. AI is not only a business opportunity. It is also a system with costs, risks, and ethical consequences.

This curriculum connects to the kind of work I want to do in the future: analyzing systems, understanding hidden costs, and thinking about how innovation can become more accountable and sustainable.
