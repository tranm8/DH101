# Human + AI Statement: What Does It Mean to Be Human in the Age of AI?

What does it mean to be human in the age of AI? After taking DH101: Critical Making in the Age of AI, my answer is that being human means more than simply using advanced tools. It means making, questioning, revising, caring, and taking responsibility for the systems we help create. AI can write, draw, code, summarize, and generate new ideas, but it does not remove the need for human judgment. In fact, the more powerful AI becomes, the more important human responsibility becomes. My central argument is that being human in the age of AI means refusing to become a passive user of technology. Instead, we must become critical makers who understand that digital tools are connected to identity, labor, power, bias, and the environment.

One of the most important ideas I learned in this course is that making is not only about producing a final object. In Matt Ratto’s idea of “critical making,” the process of making is also a form of thinking. This changed how I understood my weekly projects. At first, I thought a “make” was just a creative assignment, such as a selfie, comic, GIF, map, or game. But over time, I realized that each artifact was also an argument. When I created an AI selfie, I was not only making an image. I was asking what authenticity means when identity can be generated or remixed by a machine. When I worked on a comic or game, I was not only using digital tools. I was thinking about authorship, choice, and whether AI acts as a tool, collaborator, or something more complicated.

The readings also helped me see that AI is not neutral. Bender and Gebru’s “On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots” shows that large language models can produce fluent language without real understanding. This helped me think more carefully about AI-generated text. Just because an AI answer sounds clear does not mean it is accurate, ethical, or meaningful. In my own work, I saw this when AI gave suggestions that sounded polished but sometimes felt too general or disconnected from my real project. This taught me that human interpretation still matters. AI can help generate possibilities, but humans must decide what is useful, truthful, and responsible.

Another major lesson from the course is that AI systems are shaped by power. D’Ignazio and Klein’s Data Feminism helped me understand that data is never neutral. Data reflects the choices, values, and inequalities of the people and institutions that collect and use it. This became important in my mapping and network projects. When I mapped AI infrastructures, I began to see that AI is not just located in “the cloud.” It is connected to companies, data centers, labor sites, natural resources, and global systems of profit. A map or network can reveal relationships that are usually hidden, but it can also obscure people and experiences if the data is incomplete. This made me more aware that visualization is powerful, but it must be interpreted critically.

The environmental side of AI also changed my understanding of what it means to be human. Kate Crawford’s Atlas of AI argues that artificial intelligence depends on material resources, energy, land, labor, and extraction. Before this course, I often thought about AI as something digital and invisible. But through projects about AI ecology and environmental impact, I learned that every digital system has physical costs. Data centers need electricity and water. Devices require minerals. AI development depends on global supply chains. This means that using AI responsibly also means asking environmental questions. Who pays the cost of convenience? What resources are being used? What kinds of futures are we building?

The course also made me think about invisible labor. AI can appear automatic, but many people contribute to its operation, including data labelers, content moderators, miners, engineers, and users whose data becomes part of training systems. When we only focus on the final AI output, we may forget the human labor behind it. This connects to one of the strongest lessons of the semester: technology often hides the people who make it possible. Being human in the age of AI means making that labor visible and refusing to treat digital systems as magic.

My website portfolio is one way of practicing this responsibility. I included attribution boxes because I wanted to show which tools I used, what AI helped generate, and what I created or edited myself. This kind of transparency matters because AI can blur the boundary between human and machine work. Instead of pretending that I made everything alone, I wanted to document the collaboration honestly. At the same time, I also wanted to show that AI did not replace my thinking. I made choices, revised ideas, tested links, selected artifacts, and connected my projects to course themes.

## Ethical Considerations

Using AI responsibly means asking what it hides, who benefits from it, and who may be harmed by it. In my projects, I tried to document my AI use honestly, make hidden labor visible, and connect digital creativity to environmental and social responsibility.

In the end, I do not think being human in the age of AI means rejecting technology completely. It also does not mean accepting every new tool without question. To be human in the age of AI means living in the tension between creativity and critique. It means using AI when it helps, but also asking what it hides, who it benefits, and who it harms. It means remembering that speed is not the same as wisdom, and generation is not the same as understanding. Through critical making, I learned that humans still have an essential role: to question systems, care about consequences, and imagine better futures. AI can generate, but humans must take responsibility for meaning.
