## Research Notes 

This research project synthesizes the environmental metrics of modern artificial intelligence with
the historical and socio-technical frameworks established by Marie Hicks. I relied primarily on
the Nature (2024) scientific report to establish the material baseline for carbon and energy
consumption. For example, training a single model like GPT-3 emits 552 tons of CO2 and
consumes approximately 500ml of water for every brief interaction. By integrating these
quantitative metrics with Crawford and Joler’s Anatomy of an AI System, I was able to map the
global supply chain back to the artisanal mines of the Democratic Republic of Congo.
The core of my analysis applies Hicks' (2019) concept of "Invisible Labor" to modern mineral
extraction processes. Just as early computing infrastructure relied on the unacknowledged work
of female punching pools, today’s high-tech industry depends on millions of laborers in the
Global South whose physical toll remains hidden behind sleek digital interfaces. My research
argues that the environmental collapse in these regions is a "Systemic Feature" designed to
externalize costs and maintain the ethereal illusion of the Cloud for wealthy nations.

What was hardest to find: The most difficult data to obtain involved the specific resource
consumption of Tier-2 and Tier-3 suppliers within the hardware network. Major tech firms often
classify their electricity and water usage as proprietary trade secrets, which effectively obstructs
the ability of researchers to link a specific AI prompt to a specific ecological scar. Tracing the
full lifecycle reveals a profound temporal disconnect between minerals formed over billions of
years and hardware that becomes e-waste in less than ten years.

What surprised me: I was deeply shocked by the "Materiality Gap" between how we perceive
and how we produce technology. While users perceive AI as an abstract mathematical entity, its
physical infrastructure requires thousands of miles of cables and tons of earth excavated for a
few grams of Cobalt or Lithium. This research confirms that modern algorithmic convenience is
built upon a foundation of intense physical extraction and systemic exclusion.

## Design Statement

I chose a Digital Zine format to bridge the gap between the abstract "Cloud" and the concrete
"Soil" of our planet. The multi-page narrative arc is designed to move the reader from the clean
aesthetics of user interfaces to the gritty reality of mineral mines, forcing a confrontation with the
"Material Weight" of digital interactions. My primary goal is to apply Hicks’ concept of
"Hacking the Cis-tem" to the environmental and economic domain. By visualizing the "Invisible
Labor" of miners, I aim to break the systemic assumption that AI is a clean and frictionless tool.

The design utilizes high-contrast visuals such as deep reds representing blood and minerals
against cool digital blues to illustrate the Systemic Bias inherent in the global tech economy. As
an Economics and SES student at Denison, I designed this for our campus community to
challenge the "Green Paradox". The Zine highlights how the benefits of AI are concentrated in
wealthy regions while the ecological burdens are pushed onto marginalized communities in the
Global South.

By internalizing these economic externalities through visual storytelling, the project demands
that users move from passive consumption to critical inquiry. The final page serves as a call to
action, urging the university to move beyond green metaphors and demand total supply chain
transparency. My responsibility is to ensure that the Cloud is no longer seen as a digital vacuum,
but as a physical system connected to every scar on the earth. This Zine represents a political act
of making the invisible visible.
