1. The Protagonist’s Mental Descent
Gilman structures the story as a series of journal entries, which allows readers to witness the narrator’s psychological unraveling from the inside.
At the beginning, her voice is relatively rational, even playful. She questions the treatment but still trusts her husband’s authority.

Gradually, her writing becomes fragmented, obsessive, and repetitive. She fixates on the wallpaper’s patterns.

By the end, she fully identifies with the woman she believes is trapped behind the wallpaper, showing a complete break from reality.

Rhetorically, Gilman uses:
Unreliable narration to blur the line between reality and hallucination.

Short, choppy sentences later in the story to reflect mental instability.

Repetition of words like “pattern” and “creep” to mirror obsession.

The descent is slow and intimate—we experience madness as she does.

2. Symbolism of the Yellow Wallpaper
The wallpaper is the central symbol of the story.
The color yellow suggests sickness, decay, and discomfort. It reflects her deteriorating mental state.

The chaotic pattern represents confusion and lack of control. She cannot “read” or make sense of it—just as she cannot control her life.

The trapped woman behind the wallpaper symbolizes the narrator herself—and, more broadly, women confined by society.

When she tears down the wallpaper, it seems like liberation. But it is liberation through madness. The symbol becomes tragic: freedom is only possible through psychological collapse.

3. Theme of Patriarchal Control
The story critiques 19th-century gender roles and medical practices.
Her husband, John, is both spouse and physician, giving him double authority.

He dismisses her feelings as “nervousness” and forbids her from writing.

She is infantilized—called “little girl” and treated like a child.

This reflects patriarchal control:
Women’s voices are silenced.

Men define what is “rational.”

Female creativity is suppressed.

Writing secretly becomes her only act of resistance.

4. Tone Shift Throughout the Story
The tone changes significantly:
Beginning – Light, ironic, slightly humorous. She seems self-aware.

Middle – Uneasy and suspicious. The wallpaper becomes disturbing.

End – Frantic, obsessive, triumphant yet terrifying. The tone feels chaotic and detached from reality.

The final scene is especially powerful: her voice sounds victorious, but readers recognize it as complete psychological collapse. The tone moves from subtle critique to full horror.

Overall Interpretation
Gilman uses psychological realism, symbolism, and shifting tone to expose the damaging effects of patriarchal oppression. The narrator’s madness is not simply illness—it is a reaction to enforced silence and confinement.
The story suggests that when women are denied autonomy and expression, the consequences are devastating.

## Reflection

When we let machines read for us, we gain a bird's-eye view  of the story but lose the human heartbeat. Using Voyant Tools is a method called distant reading. Instead of reading every sentence for its meaning, the machine looks at the text as a giant pile of data. It showed me that "John" is the most frequent word, appearing 43 times. This gives us objective proof of his control, so we can actually see his dominance on a graph. However, Voyant is blind in context, so it doesn't know that "John" is a person or that the narrator is scared of him, it just sees a string of letters.

​In contrast, GPT performs a qualitative synthesis. This means it doesn't just count words, it looks for the vibe and the deeper message. It can explain symbolism, like how the yellow wallpaper represents the narrator being trapped by society's rules. GPT understands subtext, which are the things the author suggests but doesn't say directly.

The main difference is their definition of meaning. For Voyant, meaning is statistical density: the more a word appears, the more important it is. For GPT, meaning is semantic connection: how words link together to tell a human story. By using both, we see the story as both a mathematical pattern and an emotional journey.
