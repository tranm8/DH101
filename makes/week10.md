# The AI Boss Interview

*An interactive Twine game about leadership, automation, profit, and human accountability in an AI-driven corporate world.*

---

## The Make

This interactive Twine game places the player in the role of a CEO candidate being evaluated by an AI boss. The player must make difficult corporate decisions that balance profit, public trust, job creation, and accountability.

## My Take

This project helped me understand that games are different from static stories because they make ideas felt through rules, choices, and consequences. If I wrote *The AI Boss Interview* as a regular essay or short story, I could still discuss ethics, labor, and AI governance, but the reader would not experience those tensions in the same way.

In the game, the player moves through pressure, uncertainty, and consequence. The player cannot win by being purely profit-driven, but they also cannot win by refusing every difficult decision. The system pushes the player toward a more complex position: AI can assist leadership, but it cannot replace human accountability.

Working in Twine showed me how powerful simple branching can be. I originally thought the project only needed a few short decisions, but I realized that meaningful branching matters more than having many links. Adding press reactions and board reactions helped each choice feel more real and showed how public image, corporate power, and ethics are always connected.

This project also made me think more critically about AI collaboration. AI was useful for brainstorming, revising wording, and identifying weak choices. However, the most important work was deciding what argument I wanted the game to make and which revisions fit that argument.

## Design Statement

*The AI Boss Interview* is an interactive Twine narrative about leadership in an AI-driven corporate world. The player takes the role of a CEO candidate being evaluated by an AI boss. At first, the interview appears to be about business skill and fast decision-making, but the deeper question is whether leadership should be reduced to optimization, speed, and data.

The game asks: **when AI systems become more powerful and persuasive, what kinds of judgment should remain human?**

Rather than presenting the player with simple “good” and “bad” options, the game challenges the player to balance four key values: **profitability, public trust, job creation, and accountability**. The player faces choices such as whether to remove a forest for short-term gain, misuse state funds, or keep an efficient but racially biased hiring algorithm.

The final choice — whether to turn over all major corporate decisions to AI — is the most important moment in the game because it makes the central argument clear. To succeed, the player must reject both total inaction and mindless obedience to automation.

The mechanics support this theme through procedural rhetoric. The rules reward players who treat AI as a tool for analysis rather than a replacement for moral and political judgment. Players lose if they avoid all risk, but they also lose if they choose efficiency without ethics. In this way, the branching structure argues that leadership is not about always maximizing profit or always avoiding harm. It is about accepting responsibility for complex decisions.

This design was influenced by ideas from *Critical Making in the Age of AI*, especially the idea that Twine can be used to build arguments through simulation and choice, not just tell a story. I wanted the structure of the game itself to show that human judgment still matters in the age of AI.

## Attribution & AI Use

### Tools Used

Twine, ChatGPT, GitHub Pages, VS Code, and Markdown.

### What AI Helped Me Do

AI helped me identify weak or “fake” choices in my early Twine draft. It also helped me brainstorm stronger consequences after major decisions, structure Twine passages and endings more clearly, and adjust grammar and wording.

### What I Kept

I kept the core concept of an AI interviewing a human for a CEO role. I also kept my central theme: the tension between profit, ethics, and human judgment. The final game reflects my own interest in how AI changes leadership, responsibility, and decision-making.

### What I Changed

I revised several branches after receiving feedback. I changed choices that felt too simple, added more consequences after major decisions, and made the endings more distinct. I also added press reactions and board reactions to make each choice feel more meaningful.

### My Policy on AI Use

I believe AI is most useful for brainstorming, editing, and critique. It can help me notice gaps, but it should not replace my own argument, voice, or design choices. For this assignment, I used AI to support revision and reflection while keeping the project’s final direction and meaning under my control.
