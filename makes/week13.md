## Context Statement

This artifact originates from the year 2055, an era following the "Great Transparency Revisions"
of the 2040s. After decades of ecological collapse and supply chain scandals in the Democratic
Republic of Congo, global regulators passed the "Mandatory Material Disclosure Act." This
legislation ended the era of the "Cloud" as a weightless metaphor, forcing every AI provider to
provide a granular accounting of the physical costs associated with their models.

The present trends leading to this future are rooted in the "Surveillance Capitalism" and
"Accumulation by Dispossession" of the 2020s. In 2026, companies like NVIDIA and Google
treated resource consumption and energy demand as proprietary trade secrets. However, in this
2055 future, the "Invisible Labor" of data labelers and mineral miners has been made legally
visible through standardized certification. This certificate reveals a society that has successfully
"Hacked the Cis-tem" by internalizing environmental externalities as mandatory economic
factors.

This future assumes a fundamental shift in human-AI relations: from "Passive Consumption" to
"Ethical Stewardship." The AI is no longer a magic box; it is a resource-intensive tool that the
user must account for. This shift was necessitated by the "Materiality Gap" finally becoming too
wide to ignore as freshwater systems in data center hubs began to fail in the 2030s. This artifact
depicts an "in-between" world where technological advancement is strictly governed by the finite
physical limits of the Earth. It reveals that the only way for AI to survive was to abandon the
myth of infinite growth and embrace total supply chain accountability.

## Critical Reflection

Under the framework of "Critical Making," this artifact is not merely a forecast, but an act of
making to critique the present. It exposes the fact that in 2026, we are living in a state of
"systemic denial." We interact with AI as if it is made of pure math, willfully ignoring the "scars
on the earth" and the "poisoned water" required to sustain our digital queries.

This future is desirable because it replaces "Systemic Bias" with "Radical Accountability."
Listing specific data, such as "15g of Cobalt" from the Tenke Fungurume Mine or "38.5 hours of
invisible labor," forces the present-day viewer to confront the material reality of technology.
According to Matt Ratto’s theory, the process of creating this artifact serves as a way of
"thinking through making," transforming abstract economic concepts into tangible evidence.

To reach this 2055 future and avoid a total "Dystopian" ecological collapse, our regulatory
framework must change immediately. We must move beyond "green metaphors" and demand
that Big Tech corporations implement total mineral transparency and 24/7 carbon accountability.
This future reveals that our current "convenience" is subsidized by the suffering of marginalized
communities in the Global South. The solution to AI's problems is not better code, but better
economic and environmental ethics.
