# The Cloud Has a Body

*A visual research artifact about AI, ecology, extraction, water, energy, and the hidden material costs behind the “cloud.”*

---

## The Make

This artifact visualizes AI as a material system rather than a weightless digital service. It connects the everyday use of AI to resource extraction, data centers, water consumption, energy demand, labor, and electronic waste.

## My Take

This project made me think about AI not as a clean digital miracle, but as a physical system built from land, minerals, water, energy, and labor. Before this project, it was easy to imagine AI as something that happens only on a screen. However, researching AI’s environmental footprint showed me that every prompt depends on a much larger infrastructure.

The environmental cost that concerns me most is irreversible resource extraction. We are consuming minerals that took billions of years to form for hardware devices that may last less than ten years. This feels especially unjust because the benefits of AI are often concentrated in wealthy regions, while the pollution, labor exploitation, and ecological damage are pushed onto marginalized communities in the Global South.

This Make connects directly to my larger argument: AI is not only a question of intelligence, creativity, or automation. It is also a question of environmental justice. The “cloud” is not actually weightless. It has a body, and that body is made from mines, factories, servers, cooling systems, workers, and waste.

## Research Documentation

My research synthesizes data from two main academic sources: the *Nature* scientific report on AI’s environmental footprint and *Anatomy of an AI System* by Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler. I also used carbon and water metrics from studies by Strubell and Li et al. These sources were useful because they connect AI to both measurable environmental costs and broader social systems.

The *Nature* report helped me understand AI’s carbon emissions, water use, and energy demand. Crawford and Joler’s project helped me understand AI as a material system built through mining, manufacturing, labor, data extraction, and waste. Together, these sources allowed my project to move beyond the common “cloud” metaphor and reveal the physical infrastructure behind AI.

The hardest data to find was the actual resource consumption of specific data centers. Major technology companies often treat water and electricity usage as private corporate information, which makes it difficult to calculate local ecological impact. Tracing the full supply chain from informal mines to e-waste sites was also difficult because many parts of the process are hidden by subcontractors and limited transparency.

There are also major uncertainties in measuring AI’s total environmental impact. For example, it is difficult to compare the footprint of AI training with the footprint of daily inference, or everyday user queries. Even if one AI task seems small, billions of interactions can create a much larger cumulative impact. Because of this, my project does not claim to calculate the exact total footprint of AI. Instead, it visualizes the hidden systems that make AI possible and asks who benefits from AI and who bears its environmental costs.

## Curatorial Statement

Can AI be sustainable? My answer is no, if we continue the current “extract and discard” economic model. Sustainability would require a fundamental shift: recyclable hardware, carbon-free energy, transparent reporting, reduced water use, and stronger responsibility across the entire life cycle of AI technologies.

This means companies should be accountable not only for the final product, but also for mineral extraction, manufacturing, data center operation, labor conditions, and e-waste disposal. Without this systemic change, AI will continue to appear sustainable on the surface while hiding its real costs in poorer communities and fragile ecosystems.

As an Economics and SES student, I cannot ignore that every line of AI code is connected to a scar on the earth. AI may appear clean, efficient, and invisible on the screen, but it depends on land, water, minerals, energy, and human labor. My responsibility is not simply to stop using AI, but to use it more consciously and ethically.

For me, ethical AI use means asking where materials come from, who is harmed by production, who profits from growth, and what kinds of policies are needed to make the “cloud” visible, measurable, and accountable.
