## Research Documentation

Sources Used: This research synthesizes data from two primary academic pillars: the Nature
(2024) scientific report on AI’s environmental footprint and the Anatomy of an AI System
project by Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler. I also utilized specific carbon and water metrics
from the Strubell and Li et al. studies cited within the Nature report. These sources are useful
because they connect AI to both measurable environmental costs and broader social systems. The
Nature report helps quantify AI’s carbon emissions, water use, and energy demand, while
Crawford and Joler’s project shows that AI is not only a digital technology but also a material
system built through mining, manufacturing, labor, data extraction, and waste. Together, these
sources allow my project to move beyond the common “cloud” metaphor and reveal the physical
infrastructure behind AI.

Hardest Data to Find: The most difficult data to obtain is the actual resource consumption of
specific, individual data centers. Major tech corporations often classify their water and electricity
usage as “trade secrets,” making it difficult to calculate the localized impact on specific
ecosystems. Additionally, tracing the full supply chain from informal “artisanal” mines to
e-waste dumps is obstructed by a lack of corporate transparency. Another challenge is that many
environmental impacts are spread across different locations. For example, the user may
experience AI as an instant online service, but the costs may appear elsewhere, such as in mining
regions, manufacturing sites, server farms, or disposal sites. This geographic separation makes
AI’s environmental harm harder to see.

Uncertainties: There is significant uncertainty regarding the cumulative impact of “inference,”
or daily user queries, versus “training.” While Nature suggests AI can be more carbon-efficient
than humans for specific writing tasks, the aggregate footprint of billions of daily interactions
remains unquantified. Fluctuations in the carbon intensity of local power grids also create
margins of error in emissions estimates. Because of these uncertainties, my project does not
claim to calculate the exact total footprint of AI. Instead, it visualizes the hidden systems that
make AI possible and raises ethical questions about who benefits from AI and who bears its
environmental costs.

## Artist Statement

The environmental cost that concerns me most is the irreversible "resource extraction". We are
consuming minerals that took billions of years to form for hardware devices with lifespans of
less than ten years. This injustice is most evident when AI benefits are concentrated in wealthy
regions while the burden of pollution and labor exploitation is pushed onto marginalized
communities in the Global South.

Can AI be sustainable? The answer is no, if we continue the current "extract and discard"
economic model. Sustainability requires a fundamental shift: designing fully recyclable
hardware, utilizing carbon-free energy, and internalizing environmental costs as mandatory
economic factors. This also means that companies should be responsible for the entire life cycle
of AI technologies, from mineral extraction to manufacturing, data center operation, and e-waste
disposal. Without this systemic change, AI will continue to look sustainable on the surface while
hiding its real environmental costs in poorer communities and fragile ecosystems.

As an Economics and SES student, I cannot ignore that every line of AI code is connected to a
scar on the earth. AI may appear clean, efficient, and invisible on the screen, but it depends on
land, water, minerals, energy, and human labor. My responsibility is not simply to stop using AI,
but to use it more consciously and ethically. This means questioning where its materials come
from, who is harmed by its production, and who profits from its growth. I also believe
corporations must be pushed to make their “Cloud” visible, measurable, and accountable through
stronger environmental policies, transparent reporting, and sustainable design.
